Week 4 - IL activity: CSS revision


Task 1

I am using CSS when it comes to adapting the colours of specific elements across the website to make it consistent across. I'm also adapting the space in which I want all content to lie within through restricting the "body" space. In this way, I am able to use the white space on each side, which helps to increase readability and reduce visual clutter [1]. This is important because in our day to day lives we experience more information getting thrown at us than ever, which can lead to information overload. "Information overload is a problem that is being exacerbated by the ongoing digitalization of the world of work and the growing use of information and communication technologies [2]." Thinking about my audience, it's the last thing I want to happen to them when interacting with my website. Here are some visual examples I found on Stack Exchange of why using white space on the sides is important and effective:


  • No use of white space; clutter; doesn't encourage the user to read more; overwhelming


  • Uneven; looks odd; poor distribution

  • Centered; efficient for all screens; catches the eye

    • According to Web FX, and Fitts's law, "the bigger an object and the closer it is to us, the easier it is to move to." Therefore, centering my design through the use of white space at both sides of the screen has a special meaning to my elderly users who may differ in their motor control abilities. "When web layout is located at the center of the screen, the user can interact with the browser software and operating system much more efficiently," as all information and links or menus stay within a limited area which minimises cursor movement distance.

    Additionally, upon researching further into how I want my design to actually look like, I came across retro websites t9hat inspired me. I decided I want to utilise a "retro" look for my website, as it is minimalistic and simple to use/explore. This would not only look interesting, but set my website aside from all other websites directed at the elderly available at the market. Websites that I explored are Non Os and some websites I found though the Wayback Machine, like Geocities.

    Those two designs are very different, but they both have an old-timey feel to their interface and they both primarily utilise the center of the screen. I do not want to copy those designs, but rather use CSS to create something in the middle, that encapsulates an old, simple interface, but take it clutter-free and bring a modern twist on it, while sticking to the theme.

    In order to do that, I will use CSS to format all my website's font to Helvetica, which is the most popular font in the world. "Older adults and people with low vision have less difficulty processing type faces like Arial or Helvetica. Without the serifs, itâ€™s easier to recognize characters [3]." In my eyes, the font also contributes to a sort of retro look on the site, having enlarged it quite a bit for my audience.

    On top of that, because the letters have been enlarged, I adjusted the space between the lines in all paragraphs, so that the text is easier and faster to read.

    CSS is helpful in managing the links settings and the way users can interact with them. For my website, once hovered over the links turn into darker navy blue and their underline disappears so that the user can clearly tell that this is a selectable element. Additionally, when interacting with the active link, the background changes to pink. This informs the user that the link is currently being used.

    I'm also using the in line feature to put elements next to each other in one line and style tables so that they are readable and selectable.

    Of course, there's many more uses of CSS than those, but these are important for my website now.
