Task 2: Evaluate resources


Age UK is a website that relies heavily on text and conveying of information, but does it in a well-crafted web writing manner that is senior friendly.

Evaluate your chosen website against the fourteen professional writing characteristics.

  • Accuracy: Is the text accurate, authoritative and any arguments backed by evidence?

    • Yes, the website is very accurate. I wasn't able to explore all of it because it's very vast and covers basically all aspects of senior living, but I focused on the IT part as per my topic. The website doesn't often refer to external sources when it comes to it's arguments, it just explains different aspects and definitions of things you may encounter while engaging with technology and the digital world. As to my knowledge, they are all accurate (the ones I read) and explain in simple terms what an email, Apple device, or software, etc. is. The style of writing is concise and straight to the point without detailed descriptions. Sometimes the website recommends things like anti-virus software and refers to other companies' websites like Microsoft, etc. It doesn't really explain why those specific ones are included, it just shares resources. But they are all quite good.

  • Active voice: is it used correctly or excessively?

    • The whole website is written in an active voice, mostly using "you" and encouraging users to get engaged and offering solutions to their frustrations. I don't think it's excessive, it's appropriate for the purpose of the site and addressing user personally, so that it feels like they are in control.

  • Bias: any biases and stereotypes evident in the text?

    • I haven't noticed any biases present on the website. They don't use any pronouns, which makes the text genderless and avoids bias. The website doesn't patronise the users and encourages them to do things at their own pace and their own way while offering instructions.

  • Clarity: jargon and technical terminology defined?

    • The website has a full glossary section for technical terminology related to the digital technologies mentioned on the website. In the definitions present, the writers use simpler words that the audience will understand in order to explain a term or a gadget or a service and get the readers a gist of what they are reading about without going into details.   • Conciseness: are the paragraphs wordy?

    • The website has a lot of useful information on it. To be honest, it seems a bit overwhelming to me at first because there's so many links and tabs. However, the way the website tackles that is by splitting content into small, very specific chunks on different pages. The wordiness of the website comes more from the volume of information, not really the way things are worded, as everything is quite straight forward.

  • Conversational and Smoothly Flowing Prose: does it use plain language and contractions?

    • The website avoids complex or convoluted sentences and opts of simple, easy sentences. Where appropriate, the writers use contractions like "If you'd like to shop with us..." instead of going for the longer option.

  • Correctness: does it have poor grammar or words used incorrectly?

    • No. The website is a part of a larger, professional organisation and is maintained regularly by many people taking care of its different design aspects. The grammar is perfect and all the words are used correctly and in appropriate context.

  • Parrallelism: does it appeal to our natural desire for symmetry?

    • I haven't noticed this website using that technique. This may be because it is not trying to directly sell something, as much as it is an educational resource. Instead, it uses simple sentances/statements to convey a point. For example: "Get a free weekly friendship call. We'll match you with one of our volunteers. Our service is flexible to suit the different needs of everyone who takes part." That itself, has a level of symmetry to it.

  • Positive voice: does it use downbeat words or words that have a negative connotation?

    • It uses positive voice to address the user at all times. The negative connotations are only used for negative things like scams and fraud and the technology aiding those like spyware.

  • Strong nouns and verbs: does it overuse verbs or does it make them do the hard work?

    • No, it doesn't overuse them. The website uses positive, encouraging phrases like "Youâ€™re never too old to learn something new. There are lots of classes and courses out there where you can improve specific skills or learn something new, many of which are free of charge."

  • Sentence Variety: does it avoid sentences beginning with the same word and use a variety of sentence lengths?

    • The website combines short, medium, and long sentences. The sentences themselves are varied and used to constantly propel/develop a narrative with each step; explaining what happens next or how to use certain technologies. They never begin with the same word or phrase from what I've seen.

  • Simple words: does it use more recognisable words over showy words?

    • The website uses simple words in order to explain the purpose of each tab. However, it does use further explanations a lot and repeats things on different tabs. For example: "Email is electronic mail". That said, this is what the website is meant to do. Explain different technologies and online services, as well as elaborate on what they do and how to use them. So sometimes, the website uses longer phrases to explain simple concepts.

  • Shorter paragraphs: does it keep to 8 lines max for web paragraphs?

    • Yes, the division of content is exceptional on this website. Long explanations for different services or technologies are divided into paragraphs or elaborated on in more detail in different pages/tabs using links. In this way the user is able to decide how deep they want to get into each topic, as the level of information progresses. Additionally, it enables to skim through the content faster, so that the user is not intimidated by the amount of information given.

  • Tone and voice: is the writing attitude appropriate for intended audience and purpose of the website?

    • Yes, the tone is professional and focuses on personal encouragement of the audience to keep learning and reach out. The website doesn't patronise the people willing to learn about the digital word and instead offers a helping hand and resources to keep going that make the users feel less isolated and confused. The writers explain everything in a step-by-step approach that is easy to follow and helpful for people getting started with using online services and more.

    Task 2: Create Samples of text

    Select 2 key points in your narrative and write a sample paragraph for each.

    Example text:

    "Surveillance is a growing concern of internet users. As much as anonymity online was never really a thing, the recent developments in trackers, cookies, and a range of other data-harvesting tools pose a security threat to our personal information in unauthorised hands. However, even if authorised through a "Confirm all" pop-up message button, can an individual really trust the organisations whose services are being used? The capitalist society we live in made it quite easy for the big (and now small) tech companies to get away with selling and highly profitting off of personal and behavioural information collected from users in exchange for providing them with online services that nowadays seem essential to our daily functioning. Why should you care about this? Do you know how your data is being harvested, sold, and passed on through different channels? This section will serve as your guide into what's going on behind the scenes of big tech companies like Facebook or Google. If you feel uncertain about giving out your personal information online, you have the right to withdraw consent and take charge of your personal information. Read more and learn how [link]."

    This is a sample text that could be an introduction into a specific part of the website dealing with trackers and cookies and web browsing. I think it could still be shortened or divided into chunks later on, but it's just an example for now. I think it uses simple phrases of various lengths, authorative and active voice, as well as varied sentence starters. Having done the previous two writing expercises I think I should focus more about the positive tone and attitude towards those technologies and mention more about the benefits of using them. The paragraph would benefit from more uplifting words and a call to action earlier on. I think the terminology should also further be explain, but I can manage that by adding a glossary section and referring to that whenever needed. That said, I though about not including this section in my website at all, but I think it's unfair to my audience to not mention anything about this phenomenon and allow them to blissfully agree to sharing all their data with third-party companies because the use of those services brings them some benefit - especially when you can just decline, while getting all the benefits. That said, I will come back to re-writing this paragraph in a more positive tone later.

    Example text:

    "Social networking sites like Facebook help you stay connected with family and friends, follow public figures, and meet like-minded people. That said, it's still crucial to control your privacy settings, restricting access to personal information and refraining from sharing personal details like your phone number or address."

    I think this is a simple and effective way of presenting a key argument and calling the users to action/giving useful instructions. It uses very simple words, it's not wordy, and the tone is positive. The paragraph lets the verbs do the hard work and it encapsulates the purpose of the website without going into specifics.

    I discussed those samples with my boyfriend's mother, as she is a native speaker from Scotland and a senior University professor. She liked both and thought the content was interesting, but advised me to limit down the word count and keep it as concise and straight to the point as possible when it comes to content introductions.
