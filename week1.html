Task 3



Topic title:

Elderlearn - a website aimed to help the UK elderly to learn about surveillance capitalism, big data, privacy, and security online.

Brief summary of topic:

Surveillance is a growing concern of internet users. As much as anonymity online was never really a thing [1], the recent developments in trackers, cookies and a range of other data-harvesting tools pose a security threat to our personal information in unauthorised hands. However, even if authorised through a "Confirm all" pop-up message button, can an individual really trust the organisations whose services are being used? The capitalist society we live in made it quite easy for the big (and now small) tech companies to get away with selling and highly profitting off of personal and behavioural information collected from users in exchange for providing them with online services that nowadays seem essential to our daily functioning. That said, elderly people are the ones most pressured to adapt to modern, digital age of living that is much different to what they were used to in their youth [2]. The rapid development of technology and the legislation that follows is difficult to keep up with for the demographic known to be slowest to follow trends and engage with said tech. This unfortunately drives the increased feeling of loneliness or anxiety for them that can be omitted through providing access to tools focused on spreading awareness and understanding of the processes underlying the capitalistic model of online data collection [3]. This project is aimed to help the older adults and elderly not only adapt to living in a digital world fuelled by a new economic order, but to educate and provide them with the resources or content neccessary to stay in control of tech and personal data, as well as to manage privacy in digital age.

Task 1 (from Week 1 IL task sheet)



  • Who would benefit from engaging with this topic?

    Overall, everyone who uses the internet, orders things online, browses the web and uses devices like Alexa or social media. This is because in order to use those services you have to accept either cookies or terms and conditions, which almost always include paragraphs relating to data privacy and processing. This includes a list of not only what kind of data will be collected about you through your interactions with the service, but also how this data is then used to provide those services and how its sold to other companies in order for them to appeal to you as well or to improve. This data can be anything starting from your name and home adress to what items you search for, have in your cart, or how long you view each product or take to type in your search. Therefore, the benefit for people engaging with my topic/website would be gaining awareness of this phenomenon and help them to not mindlessly accept all cookies because a pop-up tells them to and makes it easy to continue browsing with a "Confirm All" button. A lot of people do not know about how their data is being managed behind the scenes, but it's even harder to imagine for seniors who are often pressured by their grandkids or friends and family to use different technologies they know nothing about. As much as their grandkids may teach them for example how to use a computer, they probably wouldn't mention anything about cookies, trackers, or the fact that any personal data they give out online (for example on messenger apps to talk to their grandkids) could be sold to companies they indirectly and unknowingly authorised. No one reads terms and conditions due to inconvenience of doing so to our personal time, so an outlet that is simple and straight-forward - like the website I am going to create - would spread awareness of how users fuel the exploitative economy like surveillance capitalism online. That said, as much as the website will be focused on helping the elderly understand their nature of citizenship online, the website will be accessible to everyone who is willing to learn.

    Another benefit from engaging with this topic would be to learn how to benefit from big data and how this surveillance we experience online on daily basis is not always bad. How it provides essential services to us, relevant recommendations that make browsing much easier because the algorithm knows what we are looking for, or the most efficient ways that we can get home from wherever on map services - without re-entering our address or the adresses of recent places we visited. The elderly could especially benefit from voice-operated smart technologies such as Alexa, who can set an alarm, order a groceries delivery, and manage the house activities like light or heating if connected to special equipment in the house. Therefore, the benefits of engaging with the topic I chose are broad, but there is many nuances worth discussing here. The project aims to address both pros and cons of how big data can influence the lives of the elderly through online services they use.


  • Also think about whether your envisioned audience would be acquainted with some aspects of the topic. Are they new to the topic but eager to learn? Will you have to persuade them that this is worth learning?

    I think a lot of people "understand" cookies to a certain extent. But for the purpose of this website I am going to assume that my audience is completely new to the topic. There are similar websites, such as Seniors Guide to Computers, but they don't talk about security online or how their activity is tracked and can potentially be exploited later on. My website will assume that the users already have beginner to intermediate knowledge of different technologies that require internet login and that they use them in their personal lives. The website would be for older adults and elderly who want to learn more about security online and how to protect, but also benefit from online services at the same time. I think a lot of people look out for an outlet like that to help their grandparents or older family be aware and in control of their own data online, but my website would need marketing. With good marketing, I think a lot of users from my specific audience demographic would be interested to test it out and use it. Maybe even use segments of the website for workshops with the elderly willing to learn, as Age UK already provides similar services that are really successful. They cover things like scams and fraud, but don't offer a course on how various data online is harvested, processed, and sold. I think this is worth learning about at any age, as everyone's privacy and ability to be in charge of own peronal information matters. The main reason why a lot of people have no interest in learning about the processes of surveillance capitalism is because the benefits of accepting trackers or giving out personal information and being able to quickly access the service highly outweigh the cons of declining and not being to use a certain service. But there is a way to balance this conundrum that my website aims to help out with in an easy, simple, and accessible format, which would make the website appealing to my audience.


  • How would the topic benefit them?

    • They would know how their data is being harvested, sold, processed, and used. They could become safer online and broadened their knowledge of their citizenship in the digital world. This goes for everyone who uses online services that range from Maps, to social media like Facebook, to search engines like Google, or online markets like Amazon. All those services collect sensitive, personal information about their users that they sometimes use for more than users would think. Therefore the topic would benefit them because their real life habits are being used to fuel political economics and they are not aware of it. By being aware, they are able to not only decide about their own data and how they benefit from it, but also to educate others and spread the word on how to manage and protect their privacy or increase the feeling of security online.

    Similarily, older people who scrutinise surveillance online can learn about how tracking interactions and presence online can limit criminal activity, safeguard clients, and limit liability. The activity data can be used to authenticate users, block bots, or unauthorised users, and actually protect our privacy. It's a two-sided coin and benefits to users who engage with this topic are varied too. I really liked this article by Ben Williamson talking about how we learn from surveillance capitalism and how "policy researchers may need to start interrogating the policies encoded in the software as well as the policies inscribed in government texts". However, change comes from engaging with topics that are important to people and this topic gets lots of attention from scholars, but not from older adults/elderly over 75 whose majority (75%) were recent internet users at the time according to the Office for National Statistics. The benefits of engaging with this topic are taking control and ownership of own information and maybe even inspiring other adults to get involved in encouraging governments to introduce laws managing things like big data more, especially data collection and the system of profitting off of it. Adapting already-existing laws and minds to newfound reality and recent tech developments.
